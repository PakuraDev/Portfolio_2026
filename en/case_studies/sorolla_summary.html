The Sorolla Museum redesign is a project that was born from a heuristic analysis assignment, but I ended up rebuilding it a few months ago purely for the love of art. It's the project where I've invested the most art direction, and where I've experimented with techniques I wouldn't have thought of using before.

Now, let's summarize the project, its design and its code. I'll try not to go on too long because I might give away spoilers:

How was my Sorolla Museum redesign born and what is it?

As I said, the project was born from an assignment for my TFM (shoutout to Carla, hi if you're reading this <3), which consisted of doing a heuristic analysis of a website. We were given several options, but I clearly went for the Sorolla Museum. I mean, whichever city I go to, I visit the museum — so I couldn't pass up the chance to analyze a museum's website.

Now, for the average person: what is a heuristic analysis? The definition doesn't help much, but basically it's a usability inspection method where a UXer (or several) reviews an interface and evaluates it against a list of recognized principles (the 10 heuristics of Jakob Nielsen, which I'll let you look up on your own).

Fun fact, even though it's unrelated, Don Norman looks like Mariano Rajoy, in case you want a laugh.

Anyway, moving on. My redesign is a way to "solve" the UX problems I found on the real website, but also to pay tribute and do justice to the work of Joaquín Sorolla. I know it sounds very cliché — I mean, all I'd need now is to claim I'm Sorolla's grandson's relative, but no, I just like his work.

Design

For the design I ran into a naming issue, whether cultural or just in name — when combining the classic with the modern, what style do you have? On one hand it's editorial design, but on the other it's modern and minimalist. So I did what I do best: I made up a name: "Translucent Neoclassical." I explain this name in the design section, so now you have an incentive to read it hehe.

Code

I don't want to spoil too much because the code here is the least I can talk about, but let's just say it has brutal performance (well, of course I'd say that, right? hahaha), and it's built modularly with an architecture chosen more for simplifying DX and maintenance than anything else. It's not that it's "bad," it's just an architecture that's viable for this project, but for a commercial website or one that needs SEO, it's better not to use. Anyway, I don't have much more to say — here's the link to the Lighthouse stats so you can see I'm not lying about performance. Just don't say anything about mobile performance because the website isn't responsive — it's only designed for desktops (there, another spoiler).
